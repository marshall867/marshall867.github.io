CS 371p Spring 2026: Final Entry: Marshall Brandt

Part 1: Looking Back

1. How well do you feel the course actually conveyed these ideas?

I think the course was effective at teaching the basics of C++, how to use common developer tools, and the general principles of Object-Oriented Programming.

I must admit that I don't view Object-Oriented Programming as a perfect paradigm for approaching software problems, but I do appreciate the perspective I gained on what it means to follow OOP principles.

In my view, code simplicity is the most important metric. There are some cases where OOP principles make certain types of problems very simple, like iterating over subclasses that conform to the parent's abstract class shape.

However, the mechanisms required to cover the edge cases are non-trivial. Creating pure virtual destructors and special methods (defined outside class bodies) are where I started to feel like we were adding complexity, not removing it.

For many cases, the wins gained by these language features are relatively small compared to the cost of reading and writing the required boilerplate.

Despite my critiques, I think it's valuable to understand and practice using OOP design principles. Even if it isn't my favorite, it's a widespread approach to software engineering, and it will likely prepare me for working within large codebases.

2. Were there other lessons or habits you picked up that you think you’ll carry forward?

Using git as a project management tool, along with learning how to navigate C++ codebases will definitely be skills that I use in the future. There were many lessons about C++ syntax and language features that I wouldn't have known about if I didn't take this course.

3. What tool, technique, or habit required for this course surprised you by becoming something you now find valuable?

I was surprised by my appreciation for Gtest, especially for code coverage files. They show you how many times a given line was run (or if it was never run), which is helpful for designing unit tests for complete coverage.

4. Compared to your other CS classes at UT, how much did you grow in this course?

Every class is different, but this class was most helpful to me in the way that it slowly unpacked C++ syntax. The projects and assignments felt a little contrived and unrealistic - I tend to prefer working on real problems (which are not in short supply!), as fake problems tend to have less interesting design decisions.



Part 2: My Experiences

Schedule

I liked the schedule for the most part. I wish we could've gone even deeper into C++ features, even if each lesson was brief, and maybe skip the week of ethics lectures. I also found the emphasis on re-implementing arrays/vectors to be a bit tedious.

Kattis Problems

These assignments were fun. I wish we did more of these and fewer readings and blogs

Collaborative Quizzes

I commute, so if I'm not already on campus, it takes me 40 minutes both ways to come to class (80 minutes of travel for 50 minutes of class). I missed many classes, and the main reason for my average not being an A is due to quizzes and exercises I was absent for. To me, this reflects a grading scheme that prioritizes form over function. Funnily enough, it's similar to how OOP-following classes can have 100% coverage and extensive polymorphism, yet still contain bugs, while a simple approach that "breaks the rules" might be bug-free. Like anything, it's case dependent, but for me, the in-class participation requirements were my least favorite.

The inability to participate remotely seems unnecessary to me, and I think the professor's reasons for requiring attendance are not in the students' best interests.

Cold Calling

For the most part, I think cold calling is a good way to keep students on their toes and paying attention. However, there were many cases, almost every class, where the professor would call on someone who clearly had not been paying attention. These slow encounters end up slowing down class and limiting what topics we reached. I think it would be more effective if the professor quickly jumped to a new cold-call in these cases, since it inhibits the rest of the class from advancing.

Collaborative Exercises

I found these exercises to be interesting, but many of the exercises were difficult to finish on-time. I received a 0/14 because my code had a single syntax typo when the timer ended, even though I was passing at least half of the test cases moments before. I was trying to get 14/14, but ended up with 0.
