WEEK #10

This week we had guest lecturers who spent three classes going over various ethical frameworks, ethical dilemmas, and current events. It was interesting to hear a full explanation of "software ethics" - as these topics are usually only mentioned in passing. It's important to be aware of the dominant narratives in the space of ethics, especially as software becomes more and more influential in society. Every new technology creates moral panic, and as technological development accelerates, so does moral concern.

I found it interesting that at the beginning of the series, the lecturers admitted that creating a definition for what's right and what's wrong is practically impossible. Various philosophers have posited different definitions, but it's critical to remember that there isn't and can't be a set of ethical rules that everyone agrees on. Here are the different approaches that they highlighted:

  • Aristotle: Ethics is the study of what is good for the human being
  • Immanuel Kant: Ethics is the science of the laws of freedom
  • John Stuart Mill: (Utilitarianism) The creed which accepts as the foundation of morals, Utility, or the Greatest Happiness Principle, holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness
  • Potter Stewart: Ethics is knowing the difference between what you have a right to do and what is right to do

Utilitarianism was mentioned to be the most relevant in today's ethical landscape, and this aligns with the ethical justifications I've seen in the real world. It follows logically from the trend towards globalism and progressivism, as the rule of law needs to apply to everyone, so why not try to prioritize everyone's happiness?

Unfortunately, I think utilitarianism punches above its weight in terms of being a practical framework. It's very difficult to argue against from a idealistic perspective, since it claims to provide the best outcome for everyone. However, utilitarianism falls apart if predictions about the future are incorrect (expected value calculations). If a law or judgement is made based on an expected value calculation, that calculation holds a lot of power. One of the most proven points in recent history is that technology and current events are extremely unpredictable. To me, this is reason enough to doubt any claims that rely on utilitarianism as justification.

One of the most utilitarianism-inspired organizations is Effective Altruism. The idea behind it is to allocate charity in the most effective way possible, by minimizing the expected value of suffering. Effective Altruism has a very strong pitch, and it's not surprising that it has attracted a lot of smart minds to advocate for it.

However, any time you enshrine a label or set of rules, the edge cases reveal themselves. When trying to decide on what counts as sufferring worth minimizing, things start to get weird: Shrimp Welfare

Effective Altruism has also been a large advocate for AI Safety ("Doomerism"). These ideas sound noble at first, but when you observe the effects of their protests and fear-mongering, it begins to lose its appeal. I don't want to get into specifics, but I've been following the feud between Effective Altruism and e/acc (effective accelerationism), and I find e/acc to be much more grounded in practicality and prioritizing bottom-up freedom instead of top-down control. "Just Stop AI" claims that stopping AI progress until we know how it works is the safest option, but it fails to consider the costs of delaying technology that can be used for massive improvements to living conditions for tons of people.

Ethics are a very contentious topic, and as technology gets more powerful, more and more people will want to shape technology to fit their moral compass. I see rigid ethical frameworks as leaky abstractions, pushing complexity onto the messy reality of individual decisions. It's important to be aware of these trends and ideals, and it's important to be aware of the potential consequences of technologies and the decisions around them.

Pick of the week

e/acc

e/acc is an interesting ideology that combats the moral panic around AI and technology. Even if it's not something you agree with, I think it's important to be aware that there are always two sides to any ethical framework
